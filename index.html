The cataloguing of information about the traditional uses of plants has long been an extractive and violent project of empire. For almost 2000 years, Dioscorides' work De Materia Medica was the widest read record of traditional European use of medicinal plants. Dioscorides was a military doctor, and his work was based on interviewing local villagers as the army he accompanied pillaged across the countryside. Locally, the textbooks of the Eclectic Herbal Doctors of 19th and 20th century Turtle Island contain vast empirical clinical records of medicinal use of native plants. The Eclectics, a reformist wing of the 19th century Popular Health Movement, remained whisper-quiet about the likely sources of their information, within the context of an epoch-defining indigenous genocide. Today, American scientists and pharmaceutical industry reps comb through what's left of distant forests, in search of rare species in profitably-limited supply, to sell as extracts to the sick and dying at the highest price the richest of them can afford.
